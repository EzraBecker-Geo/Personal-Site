Cutting and Shaping Rocks

I use a variety of tools, including saws, grinders, and rotary devices, to shape rocks for diverse purposes.

Working with Local Meta Andesite

My backyard is filled with exceptionally hard meta andesite boulders, which present a significant challenge to work with. The Carolina terrane originated as a volcanic island arc during the late Precambrian to early Paleozoic, formed above a subduction zone in an oceanic setting. It collided and accreted to the eastern margin of Laurentia, contributing to the formation of the Appalachian Mountains.

Here are some of the things I've done with the rocks from my yard:

This project is an alcohol burner for ambiance. I first took a boulder and cut it into a 3" thick slab. Then I applied an O-shaped template and carved out a trough to a depth of 1".

I needed a weight to operate a jack spit (rotisserie) that I designed and built. Instead of purchasing metal stock, I opted to use meta andesite for the project. However, crafting a rock cylinder with basic tools proved to be a challenging task.

As a tribute to my home state, rich in geological wonders, I carved the shape of North Carolina from this rock.

Here are some of the other materials I've used for projects.

Here is a mica-garnet schist that I cut to be used as a weight to drive a 3D-printed clock. This is by far the softest rock I have ever cut and polished.

A friend brought me this pegmatite, containing small crystals of schorl and almandine embedded in a quartz matrix, which he found in Western North Carolina. Before cutting this rock it was hard to tell exactly what it was.

There is an area in my town where I frequently find bits of siderite similar to this sample I cut and polished.

This is a custom marker for the number 13 tee pad of a disc golf course. I carved a relief into a concrete paver and filled it with garnet and peridot for added detail and texture.
